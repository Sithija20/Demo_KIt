Experiments and Demonstrations

Perform these experiments using the Mechanism Demonstrator Kit to gain hands-on understanding of mechanical principles.

Experiment 1: Gear Characteristics (Involute vs. Cycloidal)

Objective

To observe the difference in meshing between 'Involute' and 'Cycloidal' gears and the effect of changing 'Center Distance'.

Components Required

  • Base Plate
  • Shafts
  • Involute Gear Pair
  • Cycloidal Gear Pair
  • Mechanism for adjusting Center Distance

Procedure

  1. Assemble the 'Involute' gear pair on the base plate at the correct 'Center Distance'. Rotate the gears and observe the smoothness of meshing.
  2. Slightly increase and decrease the 'Center Distance'. Observe how the 'Involute' gears tolerate this change.
  3. Replace the 'Involute' pair with the 'Cycloidal' gear pair, ensuring precise 'Center Distance'. Observe meshing.
  4. Attempt to slightly change the 'Center Distance' for the 'Cycloidal' pair. Observe the effect (likely jamming or improper meshing).

Observations & Analysis

Record the differences in tolerance to 'Center Distance' variations. Discuss why 'Involute' gears are more commonly used in applications where precise mounting is difficult.

Experiment 2: Simple vs. Compound Gear Trains

Objective

To assemble Simple and Compound gear trains and determine their respective 'Gear Ratios'.

Components Required

  • Base Plate, Shafts
  • Assortment of Involute Spur Gears (different sizes)

Procedure

  1. Assemble a 'Simple Gear Train' using two gears. Count teeth (N1, N2). Rotate the input gear one full turn and count the output gear turns. Calculate the experimental ratio (Turns_in / Turns_out) and theoretical ratio (N2/N1).
  2. Assemble a 'Compound Gear Train' (e.g., 4 gears on 3 shafts). Count teeth (N1, N2, N3, N4 where N2 and N3 are on the same shaft). Repeat rotation counting. Calculate experimental ratio and theoretical ratio ((N2*N4)/(N1*N3)).

Observations & Analysis

Compare theoretical and experimental ratios. Discuss the difference in achievable speed reduction between simple and compound trains for a similar number of gears.

Experiment 3: Effect of Belt Tension

Objective

To measure initial 'belt tension' and observe how varying tension affects the transmission of motion (specifically slip).

Components Required

  • Base Plate, Shafts
  • Pulleys (Flat)
  • Flat Belt
  • Mechanism for adjusting pulley distance (tension adjustment)
  • Mechanism for measuring tension (e.g., spring balance)

Procedure

  1. Assemble the belt and pulley system.
  2. Set the initial tension to a low value using the adjustment mechanism. Measure and record this tension using the provided tool.
  3. Rotate the driver pulley and observe the driven pulley. Note any significant slip (driver pulley turns but driven pulley lags or stops).
  4. Incrementally increase the tension using the adjustment mechanism. Measure and record the tension at each step.
  5. Repeat the observation of slip at each tension level.

Observations & Analysis

Plot the observed slip (e.g., qualitative: None, Slight, Significant) against the measured tension. Discuss the relationship between belt tension and the ability to transmit power without excessive slip.

Experiment 4: Graphing CAM Follower Motion

Objective

To experimentally determine and 'graph' the displacement of the 'Follower' for one full rotation of the 'CAM'.

Components Required

  • Base Plate, Shafts
  • CAM
  • Follower
  • Mechanism for rotating CAM and measuring angle
  • Mechanism for measuring Follower displacement
  • Graph paper or software

Procedure

  1. Assemble the CAM and Follower mechanism.
  2. Set the CAM angle to 0 degrees (starting position). Measure and record the Follower's displacement (this might be your zero reference).
  3. Rotate the CAM by a small increment (e.g., 10 or 15 degrees). Measure and record the new Follower displacement.
  4. Repeat step 3 for one full rotation of the CAM (360 degrees).
  5. Plot the Follower displacement (Y-axis) against the CAM angle (X-axis) on graph paper or using software.

Observations & Analysis

Analyze the resulting displacement 'graph'. Identify the 'rise', 'dwell' (if any), and 'fall' portions of the follower motion. Compare the shape to theoretical CAM profiles (e.g., SHM, cycloidal) if known.
